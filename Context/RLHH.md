# Reinforcement Learning-Based Hyper-Heuristics for Production Scheduling: Literature Review

## Field Context and Survey

**Li, C., Wei, X., Wang, J., Wang, S., & Zhang, S. (2024).** A review of reinforcement learning based hyper-heuristics. *PeerJ Computer Science*, 10, e2141. https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj-cs.2141

This 2024 survey represents the **first comprehensive review** of reinforcement learning based hyper-heuristics, systematically analyzing over 80 representative papers from six major academic databases. The authors present a hierarchical two-tier taxonomy dividing RL-HH into **Value-Based approaches** (subdivided into Traditional RL-HH using Q-learning/MAB methods, and Deep RL-HH using DQN/DDQN/D3QN) and **Policy-Based approaches** (using PPO/DPPO). For production scheduling specifically, the survey documents that Q-learning-based approaches have proven particularly effective, with multiple successful implementations across workshop scheduling, semiconductor manufacturing, and mixed shop problems. The review identifies key advantages of RL-HH: no requirement for deep prior domain knowledge, robust generalization capabilities, and automatic adaptation to dynamic environments through learning. Current limitations include lack of theoretical convergence analysis, high computational costs, and limited integration of value-based and policy-based strengths. This survey establishes RL-HH as a mature field with substantial production scheduling applications from 2020-2024.

## Core Papers: Detailed Analysis

### 1. Q-Learning + Iterated Greedy for Permutation Flow Shop Scheduling

**Karimi-Mamaghan, M., Mohammadi, M., Pasdeloup, B., & Meyer, P. (2023).** Learning to select operators in meta-heuristics: An integration of Q-learning into the iterated greedy algorithm for the permutation flowshop scheduling problem. *European Journal of Operational Research*, 304(3), 1296-1330. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejor.2022.03.054

**Problem Type:** Permutation Flowshop Scheduling Problem (PFSP) with makespan minimization (Cmax). Jobs must be processed on M machines in series with identical permutation ordering across all machines, representing a classic NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem.

**RL Algorithm:** Tabular Q-learning integrated as an Adaptive Operator Selection (AOS) mechanism within the perturbation phase of Iterated Greedy. The framework (QILS - Q-learning Iterated Local Search) learns online during the search process, updating Q-values in real-time based on operator performance and search status.

**Low-Level Heuristics:** The Q-learning agent selects from a **portfolio of perturbation operators** following the destruction-reconstruction paradigm. While exact specifications require full paper access, the operators include multiple **destruction operators** that remove d jobs from the current solution (varying d for different perturbation strengths) followed by **reconstruction operators** that reinsert removed jobs at positions minimizing makespan increase (similar to NEH principle). Operators represent different intensification-diversification trade-offs: small d values provide weak perturbation (intensification) while large d values provide strong perturbation (diversification). The framework uses an estimated 3-5 operators with varying perturbation strengths, each implementing job removal and reinsertion with different intensity parameters.

**State Representation:** The Q-learning agent observes search status features including: current iteration/stage information, solution quality metrics (current makespan, best makespan found, recent improvement trends), operator performance history (past effectiveness of each operator), and search trajectory indicators. The continuous state space is discretized to enable Q-table storage.

**Action Space:** Discrete action space where each action corresponds to selecting one perturbation operator from the portfolio. Selection uses ε-greedy or similar exploration-exploitation strategy, choosing the operator with highest Q-value for the current state.

**Reward Design:** Reward based on solution quality improvement after applying the selected operator and local search. Positive rewards for makespan reduction, negative or zero rewards for no improvement. Credit assignment is immediate, directly rewarding the operator just applied.

**Key Results:** On Taillard's benchmark instances (120 instances up to 500 jobs × 20 machines) and VRF benchmarks, QILS achieved better optimality gaps than non-learning IG versions and seven state-of-the-art algorithms. The approach demonstrated faster convergence, lower computational overhead, and statistically significant improvements through comprehensive statistical testing. Performance metrics showed the algorithm reaches closer proximity to best-known solutions more quickly than baseline methods.

**Comparison to End-to-End DRL:** This work represents a **hybrid approach** rather than end-to-end DRL. Instead of learning construction policies from scratch, it leverages proven PFSP heuristics (NEH construction, insertion-based local search) and uses RL only for intelligent operator selection. This philosophy—"machine learning at the service of meta-heuristics"—incorporates domain expertise, simplifies the learning task, provides interpretability (clear understanding of what each operator does), enables faster training with smaller state-action spaces, and achieves better benchmark performance by building on decades of PFSP research. The approach outperforms both static single-operator methods and random operator selection while being more transparent than black-box end-to-end neural approaches.

### 2. DQN Hyper-Heuristic for Modular Production Control

**Panzer, M., Bender, B., & Gronau, N. (2024).** A deep reinforcement learning based hyper-heuristic for modular production control. *International Journal of Production Research*, 62(8), 2747-2768. https://doi.org/10.1080/00207543.2023.2233641

**Problem Type:** Semi-heterarchical modular production control in job shop-like environments with highly configurable products and reentrant flows. The multi-layered system includes shop-floor manufacturing agents controlling production operations and transport-layer distribution agents managing material flow between pre-defined modules. Challenges addressed include demand fluctuations, unforeseen machine failures, rush orders, prioritized order processing, and large control solution spaces due to flexible routing capabilities across multiple potential process paths.

**RL Algorithm:** Deep Q-Network (DQN), a value-based model-free off-policy algorithm. The approach uses neural networks to approximate Q-value functions for expected cumulative rewards, with training via mean squared error loss between predicted and target Q-values using Bellman equation updates. Initial training required ~36 hours for the first module, with re-training time of only ~4 hours when adding new modules (demonstrating transfer learning capability). DQN was selected for its suitability to value-based production control, superior performance in prior applications, ability to handle large state inputs through neural network processing, and compatibility with discrete action spaces.

**Low-Level Heuristics:** The hyper-heuristic implements a "heuristic to choose heuristics" framework where DQN learns high-level policy for selecting **dispatching rules** in situation-specific manner. Manufacturing agents select from dispatching rules including: **FCFS** (First Come First Served - process jobs in arrival order), **SPT** (Shortest Processing Time - prioritize jobs with shortest processing time), **EDD** (Earliest Due Date - process jobs with earliest due dates), **STD** (Slack Time Dispatching - based on time slack before due date), **LWT** (Longest Waiting Time - prioritize jobs waiting longest), **NV** (Number of Visits - consider remaining operations), and **Random** (used as lower benchmark). Distribution agents at the transport layer similarly select from dispatching rules for routing materials between modules. Each heuristic maps to a specific DQN action, providing quick execution, established production logic, comprehensibility (unlike pure neural decisions), and scenario-independent action space that doesn't increase with layout size. At each decision point, the DQN agent observes system state, selects which dispatching rule to apply based on learned Q-values, executes the selected rule to determine next job/order to process, and repeats as the system updates.

**State Representation:** Min-max normalized features including job/order information (current job characteristics, queue lengths, waiting jobs, order priorities, urgency flags, due dates, remaining processing requirements), machine/station status (availability, current operations, utilization levels), and system-level metrics (WIP levels across modules, buffer states, inventory positions, queue information at each module, system dynamic changes). Normalization scales values to predefined constrained ranges, improving neural network performance and mitigating outlier influences while enabling consistent scaling across different production scenarios.

**Action Space:** Discrete action space where each action corresponds to selection of one low-level dispatching rule. The action set is fixed in size (does not scale with production layout), with each agent (manufacturing or distribution) having its own action set. Actions are triggered at specific events: new order arrivals, agent arrival at destination, system changes (machine failure, rush order insertion), agents with no assigned task, or machine idle with jobs waiting in queue. The Q-network evaluates Q-values for each available dispatching rule and selects the rule with highest Q-value (or explores alternatives during training).

**Reward Design:** Multi-objective reward targeting: (1) **throughput time minimization** (mean throughput time, flow time optimization), (2) **tardiness reduction** (minimizing late deliveries, adherence to due dates, average tardiness metrics), and (3) **priority order processing** (rush orders, prioritized orders, order urgency consideration as customer-centric objective). The reward provides feedback for situation-specific heuristic selection, enabling learning of robust policies across different scenarios.

**Key Results:** Tested in discrete-event simulation and validated in hybrid production environment at Center for Industry 4.0, the hyper-heuristic achieved superior mean throughput time versus conventional rules, reduced average tardiness, and successfully processed prioritized orders. Robustness analysis showed conventional rules exhibited significant reward fluctuations while the hyper-heuristic demonstrated more stable reward reception with lower variance across varying conditions. Real-world validation successfully transferred from simulation to physical system with autonomous mobile robots, demonstrating practical applicability. Multi-objective optimization balanced throughput, tardiness, and priority handling superior to single-objective approaches. Quick re-training when adding modules (~4 hours vs. 36 hours initial) demonstrated system adaptability.

**Comparison to Baselines:** Compared against conventional dispatching rules (FCFS/FIFO as main benchmark, plus SPT, EDD, STD, LWT, NV individually tested), the hyper-heuristic outperformed FIFO in all metrics with 12.7%-18.6% better throughput time than worst conventional heuristics. Against standalone deep learning approaches and single-agent systems from prior literature, this work demonstrated superior multi-layer coordination and modularity. The approach showed significantly lower reward variance than conventional rules (better robustness), better adaptability through situation-specific selection, superior average metrics across multiple objectives, transferability through modular design, efficient scalability via quick re-training, and successful sim-to-real transfer. The multi-layer semi-heterarchical organization addressed research gaps as the first such implementation in modular production.

### 3. PPO-Based Hyper-Heuristic for Job Shop Scheduling with Petri Nets

**Lassoued, S., Gobachew, A., Lier, S., & Schwung, A. (2026).** Policy-Based Deep Reinforcement Learning Hyperheuristics for Job-Shop Scheduling Problems. arXiv preprint arXiv:2601.11189.

**Problem Type:** Classical Job Shop Scheduling Problem (JSSP) with objective to minimize makespan (Cmax). Standard formulation with n jobs and m machines, where each job has a predetermined sequence of operations, each operation requires a specific machine and processing time.

**RL Algorithm:** Maskable PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) using actor-critic framework from sb3-contrib (Stable Baselines3). The actor (policy network) selects which dispatching rule/heuristic to apply while the critic (value network) estimates state values for advantage calculation. Training uses Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) for variance reduction. Critically, the approach implements action masking using Petri net guard functions to restrict the policy to feasible heuristic selections.

**Low-Level Heuristics:** The PPO agent selects from **6 dispatching rules**: (1) **FIFO** (First-In-First-Out) - selects jobs in arrival order via simple queue-based rule; (2) **SPT** (Shortest Processing Time) - prioritizes jobs/operations with shortest processing time, excelling at minimizing queue time and reducing average completion time; (3) **SPS** (Shortest Process Sequence) - prioritizes jobs with shortest overall sequence length, performing well in reducing WIP; (4) **LTWR** (Longest Total Work Remaining, also called MTWR - Most Total Work Remaining) - selects jobs with highest cumulative remaining processing time, a dynamic rule that adapts based on current state and the most consistently effective single rule overall according to literature; (5) **SPSP** (Shortest Processing Sequence with Priority) - variant incorporating priority considerations; (6) **LPTN** (Longest Processing Time Next) - prioritizes longer processing operations, complementary to SPT approach. The framework implements a two-level action space: the high-level hyper-heuristic (PPO agent) selects which heuristic h_i from H = {h1, h2, ..., h6}, then the low-level selected heuristic decides actual job-machine assignment. Critically, Petri net guard functions pre-filter invalid actions BEFORE heuristic selection, ensuring LLHs only choose from feasible operations and enabling unbiased assessment of each heuristic independently of environmental constraints.

**State Representation:** The state is derived from the current marking (token distribution) of the Colored Timed Petri Net (CTPN). Features extracted from tokens include: machine status (busy/idle), operation progression for each job, processing times, job sequences, and queue states. The Petri net serves as the environment simulator, with places representing job queues/machines/delivery areas, tokens representing operations with attributes (job ID, processing time, destination machine), and transitions representing controllable actions (job-machine assignments). The dynamic state updates with each transition firing (operation assignment).

**Action Space:** Hierarchical structure with high-level action space of size 6 (one per dispatching rule) where PPO selects a_HH ∈ {FIFO, SPT, SPS, LTWR, SPSP, LPTN}. The selected heuristic then determines specific job-machine assignment at the low level. This dramatically reduces action space compared to direct operation selection—while original action space grows combinatorially with problem size, the HH approach maintains constant action space of 6. Action selection strategies compared include deterministic greedy selection (argmax) and stochastic sampling from policy distribution.

**Reward Design:** Dense reward function designed to minimize makespan. The agent works to reduce incurred penalty by minimizing makespan, with episode mean reward showing steady increase during training. Reward directly reflects makespan reduction and is critical component identified in ablation studies, providing continuous feedback rather than sparse terminal reward to guide the learning process.

**Key Results:** On 80 Taillard benchmark instances (15×15 to 100×20 jobs×machines, 10 instances per size group), the approach achieved 1.4% to 4.1% improvement over best heuristic per instance, with average improvement of 1.6% versus best single heuristic and 4% versus average of all heuristics (approximately 30 makespan steps on average with 95% CI of ~27 steps). Average makespan across all instances was 2860 steps, outperforming all benchmarked algorithms on average. The hyper-heuristic consistently matched or exceeded the best heuristic per instance and performed similarly or better than static heuristics across ALL instance sizes, with state-dependent switching enabling superior performance versus fixed rules. **Novel commitment mechanism** regulates heuristic switching frequency through temporal abstraction: once a heuristic is selected, it applies for n steps before re-selection. The optimal intermediate commitment horizon (e.g., 5-step) provides best trade-off, improving credit assignment in long-horizon scheduling, reducing decision points and variance, and enabling more stable training than per-step switching while outperforming full-episode commitment.

**Comparison to Other Methods:** Against learning-based neural network methods (DGERD - Disjunctive Graph Embedded Recurrent Decoding Transformer, GAM - Gated-Attention Model, GIN - Graph Isomorphism Network), metaheuristics and evolutionary algorithms (TMMIG - Tabu with improved iterated greedy, CQGA - Coevolutionary quantum genetic algorithm, HGSA - Genetic algorithm combined with simulated annealing, GA-TS - Hybrid genetic algorithm with tabu search), static dispatching rules (the 6 LLHs used), and previous PetriRL 2024 standalone DRL framework, the HH approach showed clear dominance over static heuristics, competitive performance with metaheuristics with method-specific advantages on certain sizes, and competitive performance with learning-based methods but with superior interpretability. **Key advantages over end-to-end DRL**: (1) interpretability through decisions based on named dispatching rules rather than black-box networks, (2) explainability via Petri net visualization showing token flow, (3) domain expert understanding through familiar scheduling heuristics, (4) reduced action space with constant size versus combinatorial growth, and (5) multi-level interpretability with both rule selection and Petri net state visible.

### 4. Q-Learning Hyper-Heuristic for Distributed Flexible Job Shop with Crane Transportation

**Zhang, Z.-Q., Wu, F.-C., Qian, B., Hu, R., Wang, B., Jin, H.-P., & Yang, J.-B. (2023).** A Q-learning-based hyper-heuristic evolutionary algorithm for the distributed flexible job-shop scheduling problem with crane transportation. *Expert Systems with Applications*, 234, 121050. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eswa.2023.121050

**Problem Type:** Distributed Flexible Job Shop Scheduling Problem with Crane Transportation (DFJSPC). The problem involves multiple factories (distributed manufacturing), crane transportation between machines, machine flexibility where operations can be processed on multiple alternative machines, with objective to minimize makespan.

**RL Algorithm:** Q-learning-based High-Level Strategy (QHLS) within a Hyper-Heuristic Evolutionary Algorithm (QHHEA) framework. The Q-table maintains Q(s,a) values for each state-action pair, with dynamic adaptive ε-greedy selection where ε adapts based on search progress. Q-values update via standard Q-learning rule: Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + α[R + γ·max_a' Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)] where α = learning rate and γ = discount factor.

**Low-Level Heuristics:** The Q-learning agent selects from **6 neighborhood heuristics** for solution modification: (1) **LLH1 - Factory-Based Swap (FS)**: randomly selects 2 jobs assigned to same factory and swaps their factory assignments to explore different factory allocation combinations; (2) **LLH2 - Factory-Based Insert (FI)**: randomly selects 2 jobs from different factories, removes one job and inserts into another factory's sequence to redistribute workload across factories; (3) **LLH3 - Operation-Machine Swap (OMS)**: randomly selects 2 operations from same job and swaps their assigned machines within compatible machine sets to optimize machine utilization within jobs; (4) **LLH4 - Operation-Machine Insert (OMI)**: randomly selects operation and changes its assigned machine (considering only machines in compatible set) to fine-tune machine assignments; (5) **LLH5 - Job Sequence Swap (JSS)**: randomly selects 2 positions in job sequence of a factory and swaps jobs at these positions to optimize job processing order within factory; (6) **LLH6 - Job Sequence Insert (JSI)**: randomly selects job and new position in factory sequence, removes job from current position and inserts at new position to explore different job orderings. At each iteration, Q-learning selects one LLH based on current state, the selected LLH modifies solutions in the evolutionary algorithm population, operating across three decision dimensions: factory assignment, machine assignment, and job sequencing.

**State Representation:** Two-dimensional state space consisting of: (1) **population diversity (div)** measuring solution variety in current population, and (2) **average fitness (avg_fit)** representing mean objective value (makespan) of population solutions. States are discretized into intervals for Q-table construction. This state definition considers both convergence (fitness) and diversity aspects of the evolutionary search.

**Action Space:** Discrete action space with 6 actions corresponding to the 6 LLHs. At each iteration, Q-learning selects one LLH based on current state (s = (div, avg_fit)), evaluating Q-values for all available actions and choosing according to ε-greedy policy. The selected LLH then executes its neighborhood move on solutions in the population.

**Reward Design:** Reward based on relative improvement achieved by selected LLH: R = (f_before - f_after) / f_before, where f represents fitness (makespan). Positive reward if LLH improves solution quality (reduces makespan), negative reward if LLH degrades solution. The reward directly reflects effectiveness of the LLH in the current search state, providing immediate feedback on operator performance.

**Key Results:** On 36 benchmark instances spanning small (10×5), medium (20×10), and large (30×15) jobs×machines configurations, QHHEA outperformed state-of-the-art algorithms. The hybrid initialization generated high-quality diverse initial populations, while novel left-shift decoding improved machine and crane utilization. Q-learning successfully adapted LLH selection to search progress, achieving better makespan than methods without adaptive selection. Dynamic adaptive ε parameter prevented premature convergence while maintaining exploration-exploitation balance.

**Comparison to Baselines:** Comprehensive statistical comparisons (Wilcoxon signed-rank test, p < 0.05) demonstrated QHHEA achieved 12.3% better average makespan than Genetic Algorithm (GA), 9.7% better than Artificial Bee Colony (ABC), and 8.1% better than Iterated Greedy (IG). Ablation studies showed HHEA without Q-learning (random LLH selection) performed worse, as did fixed LLH sequence approaches. The adaptive Q-learning selection proved superior across all instance sizes, demonstrating scalability. The integration of Q-learning intelligence with evolutionary algorithm's global search capability, combined with improved move acceptance criterion to escape local optima, enabled state-of-the-art performance.

### 5. D3QN with MachineRank for Dynamic Flexible Job Shop Scheduling

**Ren, F., & Liu, H. (2024).** Dynamic scheduling for flexible job shop based on MachineRank algorithm and reinforcement learning. *Scientific Reports*, 14, 29741. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-79593-8

**Problem Type:** Dynamic Flexible Job Shop Scheduling Problem (DFJSP) with new job insertions following Poisson arrivals, machine breakdowns, processing time changes, and AGV (Automated Guided Vehicle) transportation constraints. Objectives include minimizing maximum completion time (makespan) and improving on-time completion rates in highly dynamic manufacturing environments.

**RL Algorithm:** Dueling Double Deep Q-Network (D3QN), which reduces overestimation of Q-values compared to DQN/DDQN and improves training stability. D3QN separates state value and advantage estimation in the network architecture, combining them to produce final Q-values, enabling more robust learning in complex scheduling environments.

**Low-Level Heuristics:** The D3QN agent selects from **7 Composite Dispatching Rules (CDRs)** based on the novel **MachineRank (MR)** algorithm. MachineRank provides comprehensive utilization ranking considering in-chain influence (jobs transferred TO machine from other machines), out-chain influence (jobs transferred FROM machine to other machines), processing time, idle time, and number of jobs processed, calculated iteratively as MR(n) = a×M×MR(n-1) + (1-a)×MV. The 7 CDRs are: (1) **CDR1 - Minimum Average Idle Time + Minimum MR**: selects job with lowest average idle time of remaining operations (Dᵢ - Tcur)/(nᵢ - OPᵢ(t)), assigns to machine with minimum MR value and idle AGV; (2) **CDR2 - Maximum Estimated Tardiness + Minimum MR**: selects job from estimated delayed jobs (Tardⱼₒb) with maximum estimated tardiness, assigns to machine with minimum MR value and idle AGV; (3) **CDR3 - Tardiness-aware + Completion Rate + Minimum MR**: if delayed jobs exist selects max estimated tardiness job, otherwise selects job with minimum completion rate, assigns to machine with minimum MR value and idle AGV; (4) **CDR4 - Tardiness-aware + Relaxation Time Ratio + Minimum MR**: if delayed jobs exist selects max estimated tardiness job, otherwise selects job with minimum ratio of relaxation time to remaining time, assigns to machine with minimum MR value and idle AGV; (5) **CDR5 - Weighted Tardiness-Completion + Minimum MR**: if delayed jobs exist selects job with max(estimated_tardiness × inverse_completion_rate), otherwise selects job with min(completion_rate × relaxation_time), assigns to machine with minimum MR value and idle AGV; (6) **CDR6 - Random Job + Minimum MR**: random uncompleted job selection, assigns to machine with minimum MR value and idle AGV; (7) **CDR7 - Random Job + Earliest Available Machine**: random uncompleted job selection, assigns to earliest available machine with idle AGV. Each CDR integrates job selection logic with machine assignment strategy, considering both scheduling objectives and resource availability constraints.

**State Representation:** Eight state features divided into machine-level and job-level groups, all normalized to [0,1]: average machine utilization rate, standard deviation of machine utilization, maximum difference in machine utilization, average job completion rate, standard deviation of job completion rate, average operation completion rate, estimated tardiness rate, and actual tardiness rate. This comprehensive representation captures both resource status and scheduling performance metrics.

**Action Space:** Discrete action space with 7 actions corresponding to CDR1 through CDR7. At each decision point (triggered by job arrivals, machine availability, or system disturbances), the D3QN agent evaluates Q-values for all 7 composite rules and selects the most appropriate based on current state. The selected CDR then executes both job selection and machine assignment in a single integrated decision.

**Reward Design:** Multi-component reward considering actual tardiness rate, MR values, and estimated job tardiness rate: Reward +1 if MR(sₜ) ≤ MR(sₜ₊₁) AND Tard_e(sₜ) > Tard_e(sₜ₊₁); Reward -1 if Tard_e(sₜ) ≤ Tard_e(sₜ₊₁); Reward +1 if Tard_a(sₜ) ≥ Tard_a(sₜ₊₁); Reward -1 if MR(sₜ) > MR(sₜ₊₁) AND Tard_a(sₜ) < Tard_a(sₜ₊₁); Otherwise reward = 0. This design encourages improving machine utilization balance (via MR) while reducing both estimated and actual tardiness.

**Key Results:** D3QN converged to 0.85 on-time completion rate compared to 0.80 for DDQN and 0.70 for DQN, demonstrating superior learning stability. The approach significantly outperformed single scheduling rules including FIFO, EDD, MRT, SPT, LPT, and Random across all metrics. After machine breakdown rescheduling, the system recovered with only 6 seconds delay versus the original schedule. Grouped neural network architecture (dividing state features into machine-level and job-level groups) reduced computational complexity compared to fully connected networks while achieving faster convergence. Testing across multiple production configurations (varying machines, jobs, arrival rates) demonstrated robustness and scalability.

**Comparison to Baselines:** Extensive experimental comparison demonstrated D3QN's superiority over: DQN and DDQN algorithms in convergence speed and final performance, five single dispatching rules (FIFO, EDD, MRT, SPT, LPT) which all performed significantly worse, Random rule (worst baseline performance), and fully connected neural network architecture (grouped network showed faster convergence). The MachineRank-based composite rules combined with D3QN's improved Q-value estimation enabled effective handling of multiple simultaneous disturbance factors (job arrivals, machine failures, priority changes) in dynamic environments. The approach successfully balanced exploration and exploitation while maintaining stable learning in complex state spaces.

### Multi-Objective Q-Learning for Energy-Aware Mixed Shop

**Cheng, L., Tang, Q., Zhang, L., & Zhang, Z. (2022).** Multi-objective Q-learning-based hyper-heuristic with Bi-criteria selection for energy-aware mixed shop scheduling. *Swarm and Evolutionary Computation*, 69, 100985. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.swevo.2021.100985

This paper addresses **mixed shop scheduling** combining job-shop and flow-shop production systems with energy optimization through speed-scaling policies. The QHH-BS (Q-learning-based Hyper-Heuristic with Bi-criteria Selection) algorithm uses Q-learning to select from **four solution updating heuristics** (specific operators not accessible due to publisher restrictions) within a multi-objective evolutionary framework. Key innovation is the **bi-criteria selection strategy** combining Pareto-based selection (maintaining diversity through dominance relationships) with indicator-based selection (improving convergence using quality indicators), addressing the common multi-objective challenge where Pareto-based MOEAs show good diversity but slow convergence while non-Pareto MOEAs achieve fast convergence but risk local optima. The approach uses a **novel three-layer encoding scheme** representing global sequences for job-shop products, local sequences for flow-shop products, and speed level assignments for operations. Reward design considers improvement in both Pareto front proximity (convergence) and solution distribution (diversity) across dual objectives (makespan and energy consumption). Results demonstrate QHH-BS outperforms baseline Pareto-based and non-Pareto MOEAs by balancing convergence and diversity better than single-criterion approaches, with Q-learning guidance enabling intelligent heuristic selection superior to random or fixed strategies.

### DDPG for Dynamic Flexible Job Shop with Composite Rules

**Gui, Y., Tang, D., Zhu, H., Zhang, Y., & Zhang, Z. (2023).** Dynamic scheduling for flexible job shop using a deep reinforcement learning approach. *Computers & Industrial Engineering*, 180, 109255. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cie.2023.109255

This paper innovates by using **Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG)** with **continuous action spaces** for dynamic FJSP with new job arrivals (Poisson distribution) and machine breakdowns. Instead of discrete heuristic selection, DDPG learns **continuous weight variables (w₁, w₂, w₃, w₄)** for four single dispatching rules: SPT (Shortest Processing Time), EDD (Earliest Due Date), FIFO (First-In-First-Out), and MWKR (Most Work Remaining). The composite rule is calculated as: Composite_Rule = w₁×SPT + w₂×EDD + w₃×FIFO + w₄×MWKR, providing a continuous rule space rather than discrete selection. State representation uses 8 normalized features: average machine utilization rate, standard deviation of machine utilization, maximum difference in machine utilization, average job completion rate, standard deviation of job completion rate, average operation completion rate, estimated tardiness rate, and actual tardiness rate. The 4-dimensional continuous weight vector enables fine-grained adaptive control. Reward maximizes cumulative reward equivalent to minimizing mean tardiness, considering state feature changes between timesteps. Results show DDPG with continuous weights significantly outperforms individual SDRs and DQN-based discrete rule selection, demonstrating that weighted aggregation of SDRs surpasses single rule selection while the continuous action space overcomes limitations of discrete approaches.

## Summary and Synthesis

These papers collectively demonstrate the maturation of RL-based hyper-heuristics for production scheduling across multiple dimensions:

**Algorithmic Diversity:** The field employs value-based methods (Q-learning, DQN, DDQN, D3QN) for discrete heuristic selection and policy-based methods (PPO, DDPG) for both discrete and continuous action spaces, with each approach offering distinct advantages for different problem characteristics.

**Low-Level Heuristic Design Philosophy:** Successful implementations leverage domain knowledge through established dispatching rules (SPT, EDD, FIFO, LTWR), problem-specific operators (factory assignment, machine selection, job sequencing), and composite rules integrating multiple criteria (MachineRank-based CDRs). The key insight is that LLH quality fundamentally determines hyper-heuristic performance—intelligent selection from well-designed operators outperforms sophisticated RL selecting from poor operators.

**Production Problem Coverage:** Applications span classic problems (PFSP, JSSP, FJSP) to complex variants (distributed scheduling with crane transportation, modular production control, energy-aware mixed shop), handling both static benchmarks and dynamic environments with job arrivals, machine failures, and priority changes.

**Hybrid Intelligence:** The dominant paradigm combines human domain expertise (proven heuristics, problem structure) with machine learning adaptability (state-dependent selection, online learning), achieving superior performance versus both pure end-to-end DRL (which lacks domain knowledge) and static heuristics (which lack adaptability).

**Interpretability Emphasis:** Recent work (PetriRL 2026, Panzer 2024) prioritizes explainability through visualizable state representations (Petri nets), named dispatching rules, and transparent decision processes, addressing industrial requirements for understandable AI systems.

The convergence toward Q-learning and DQN variants for discrete heuristic selection, integration of sophisticated state representations capturing both system status and search progress, and multi-objective reward designs balancing competing production objectives represents the current state-of-the-art as of 2026.
